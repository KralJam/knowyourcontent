The Rise of AI in News and the Growing Risk of Unchecked Information

The rapid adoption of artificial intelligence in newsrooms has transformed how information is produced, but it has also exposed a widening fault line in the quality and reliability of journalism. As experienced journalists leave the industry due to costcutting and burnout, many media organisations are increasingly relying on junior reporters who are under intense pressure to meet deadlines with fewer resources. In this environment, AI tools have become a tempting shortcut, an easy way to generate copy quickly, fill gaps in knowledge, or summarise complex topics. But without rigorous factchecking, this shift is creating a dangerous feedback loop of errors, assumptions, and unverified claims.

AI systems are powerful patternmatching machines, not truthseeking entities. They generate text that sounds plausible, not text that is fact checked for accuracy. When junior journalists use AI to draft articles, background sections, or even entire stories, the risk is not just that mistakes slip through, it’s that those mistakes are delivered with the confidence and polish of professional writing. Without experienced editors to interrogate the content, machine generated inaccuracies and complete fantasies can be published as fact, and once they enter the news ecosystem, they are often repeated, amplified, and legitimised.

For consumers, the result is a growing inability to distinguish between genuine reporting and AIassisted content. A news article may be partially AIgenerated, lightly edited by a human, or entirely written by a journalist, with little to no differentiation for the end user. This lack of disclosure erodes trust. Readers cannot tell whether a story is the product of investigative effort or a machine assembling fragments of the internet into something that resembles journalism. The line between fact and fabrication becomes blurred, not because of malicious intent, but because the production process is hidden.

The deeper issue is that AI can confidently invent details, misinterpret data, or reproduce outdated or incorrect information. When these errors appear in news articles, they carry the authority of the publication’s brand. Over time, this undermines the credibility of the entire news industry. If consumers cannot reliably tell what is true, they begin to distrust everything, including legitimate reporting. This is one way that misinformation spreads, through carelessness.

The decline in experienced journalists compounds the problem. Senior reporters bring institutional knowledge, scepticism, and the ability to recognise when something “doesn’t sound right.” Junior journalists, often working under tight deadlines and without mentorship, may not have the experience to challenge AIgenerated content or recognise subtle inaccuracies. The pressure to produce more with less encourages shortcuts, and AI becomes a crutch rather than a tool.

None of this means AI has no place in journalism. Used responsibly for transcription, data analysis, or idea generation, it can enhance reporting. But when AI becomes a substitute for expertise, verification, and editorial judgement, the integrity of the news is at risk. Transparency, rigorous factchecking, and clear editorial standards are essential if AI is to coexist with trustworthy journalism.

The future of news depends ensuring that human oversight remains at the centre of the process. We now need to have our trusted institutions disclose when they are using AI and what they are using it for. Without that, we risk a media landscape where truth is optional, accuracy is inconsistent, and the public is left to navigate a fog of information with no reliable guide.
