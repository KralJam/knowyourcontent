AI Is Rewiring Public Opinion — And We’re Letting It Happen

Social media has always been a battleground for influence, but the rise of artificial intelligence has turned it into something far more dangerous: a frictionless propaganda machine. What once required armies of trolls, coordinated campaigns, or statefunded disinformation units can now be achieved by a single individual with an AI model and a handful of cherrypicked news fragments. The barrier to manipulation has collapsed, and the public is largely unaware of how deeply AIgenerated content has already infiltrated their feeds.

The core problem is simple: AI can now produce content that looks real, sounds real, and feels real — and it can do so endlessly. A convincing fake persona can be spun up in seconds. A persuasive political argument can be generated in minutes. Entire networks of “people” can be created to amplify a narrative, reinforce each other, and manufacture the illusion of consensus. And because this content is woven into the same platforms where genuine human communication takes place, most users cannot tell the difference.

This is not speculation. Analysts have already documented AIgenerated posts circulating during major political events, shaping conversations and influencing perceptions. Researchers warn that AIdriven accounts can “flood social media, shaping opinions while posing as everyday people,” making them far more effective than the crude bots of the past. When these systems are paired with cherrypicked news — real facts stripped of context and arranged to tell a misleading story — the result is a potent form of persuasion that feels organic, grassroots, and trustworthy.

The danger is not just the content itself, but the opacity surrounding it. Consumers have no way of knowing how much of what they see is AIgenerated, AIassisted, or humanwritten. There is no disclosure, no labelling, no accountability. A post that appears to be a neighbour sharing an opinion could be an algorithm. A viral thread could be a coordinated AI campaign. A seemingly heartfelt political plea could be synthetic from start to finish. The public is left to navigate an information landscape where authenticity is optional and manipulation is effortless.

The algorithmically generated echo chambers of social media are self-reenforcing. The curated feeds designed to keep people engaged and enraged with content also create a confirmation bias that means we are becoming more divided, with more “us-vs-them” thinking, less critical thinking and people are less frequently looking for commonality. We are actively hunting for the “bad guy” scenarios and the differences that divide us. This is done unintentionally with algorithms that serve us content that we engage with, even if the engagement is through rage.

Meanwhile, regulation has not kept pace. Social media companies have little incentive to police AIgenerated content, especially when engagement, even toxic engagement, drives profit. Governments are only just beginning to understand the scale of the problem. And in the vacuum, individuals, corporations, and nation states have discovered a new, powerful tool for shaping public opinion with unprecedented precision.

The result is a crisis of trust. When people cannot tell what is real, they begin to distrust everything, legitimate journalism included. This erosion of confidence is not an accident; it is the predictable outcome of an information ecosystem where AI can generate infinite narratives and no one is required to verify them.

The threat is not that AI will replace human voices. It’s that it will drown them out.

If we want to preserve any sense of shared reality, transparency must become nonnegotiable. Platforms must disclose AIgenerated content. Regulators must demand accountability. And the public must be equipped with the literacy to recognise when they are being manipulated.

Because right now, AI is shaping public opinion in ways we cannot see, cannot measure, and cannot challenge, and that should alarm all of us.
